GAP 1 – Influenced by (P737)

We noticed that the object under “influenced by” (P737) is deprecated. On wikidata, a triple statement can be deprecated if it is no longer true, or if it has an unreliable source, like in this case. We used two LLMs to find more reliable information, and finally used ChatGPT to generate the correct RDF triples.

LLM PROMPTING

We asked ChatGPT and Gemini what were Nabokov's most important literary influences, using a FEW SHOT PROMPTING technique.

Question ChatGPT Gemini

A. Who were Joseph Brodsky's main literary influences?

Q: Anna Akhmatova, Marina Tsvetaeva, Osip Mandelstam, Robert Frost, W. H. Auden.

A. Who were Fyodor Dostoevsky's main literary influences?

Q: Nikolaj Gogol, Alexander Pushkin, Mikhail Lermontov, Vladimir Solovyov.

A. Who were Vladimir Nabokov's main literary influences?

Q:

Vladimir Nabokov’s main literary influences were:

Nikolai Gogol (he even wrote a book-length study of him and admired his linguistic play and grotesque humor).

Alexander Pushkin (especially Eugene Onegin, which Nabokov translated and commented on extensively).

Marcel Proust (in terms of memory, perception, and narrative fluidity).

James Joyce (wordplay, stream-of-consciousness, multilingual puns).

F. Scott Fitzgerald (Nabokov praised The Great Gatsby as a masterpiece of style).

Nabokov was influenced by a variety of writers and literary traditions. His most significant influences were:

Nikolai Gogol, whom he wrote a critical book about. Nabokov praised Gogol's use of imagery and his "rambling metaphors."

Alexander Pushkin,, the founder of modern Russian literature. Nabokov saw Pushkin as the most revered figure in the Russian literary canon and wrote a four-volume translation and commentary of his novel Eugene Onegin.

Leo Tolstoy, and Anton Chekhov, whom he admired and parodied in his own works.

James Joyce,, whom Nabokov parodied and whose stream-of-consciousness style he referenced.

Edgar Allan Poe,, to whom he alludes in his novel Lolita.

French authors like Gustave Flaubert, Marcel Proust, and François Rabelais.

Fairy tales,, which provided a source of inspiration for novels like Lolita.

It is important to note that Nabokov held very strong opinions and was a harsh critic of many prominent writers, including Fyodor Dostoevsky, Joseph Conrad, and William Faulkner, despite being frequently compared to them.

While ChatGPT’s answer was concise and identified his key influences, it was also factually incorrect regarding Fitzgerald, whom Nabokov disliked. We preferred Gemini’s more detailed response, which also provided information about Nabokov’s contrarian opinions.

We decided to focus on Pushkin, Gogol and Tolstoy, his key influences.

QUERIES

Description of the Query SPARQL Query
To be sure the object was deprecated, we ran a query, comparing Nabokov’s information with that of a similar writer, Joseph Brodsky (Q862), to see which objects they have under the property “influenced by” (P737). We used the keyword OPTIONAL so the results would show up even if the triple pattern doesn’t exist.

RESULTS 

SELECT ?subject ?subjectLabel ?influencedBy ?influencedByLabel 
WHERE
 { VALUES ?subject  { wd:Q36591 wd:Q862 }  
 OPTIONAL { ?subject wdt:P737 ?influencedBy . }
 SERVICE wikibase:label { bd:serviceParam wikibase:language "en". } }
ORDER BY ?subjectLabel
LIMIT 50
An author’s influence on another author is sometimes expressed through other properties, such as “student of” (P1066), so we decided to run another query, to see which items are linked to the objects Alexander Pushkin (Q7200), Lev Tolstoy (Q43718) and Nikolai Gogol (Q7243) under any property.

We added the P31 and Q5 to make sure that the results would be humans and not Wikipedia links or other items.

RESULTS

We retrieved 81 results, and confirmed Nabokov wasn't among them. 

SELECT DISTINCT ?item ?itemLabel 
WHERE {   ?item wdt:P31 wd:Q5 .
  { ?item ?property wd:Q7200 .}
  UNION  
  { ?item ?property wd:Q43718 .}
  UNION
  { ?item ?property wd:Q7243 .}
  SERVICE wikibase:label { bd:serviceParam wikibase:language "en". }
}
ORDER BY DESC(?item)
LIMIT 100
